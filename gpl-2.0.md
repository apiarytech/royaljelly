### Why Dual-License?

Dual-licensing is a business model that allows you to offer your software under both a "copyleft" license (like the GPL) and a commercial license. This has a few advantages:

*   **Community Engagement:** The GPL encourages community contributions and ensures that any improvements to your software will be shared with the community.
*   **Commercial Viability:** The commercial license provides a revenue stream for your project, allowing you to fund its development.
*   **Flexibility:** Users can choose the license that best suits their needs.

### The GPL and "Copyleft"

The GPL is a "copyleft" license, which means that any derivative works of your software must also be licensed under the GPL. This is a key feature of the GPL, and it's what ensures that your software will remain free and open source.

However, this can be a problem for companies that want to use your software in a proprietary application. They may not want to release the source code for their application, which would be required if they used your software under the GPL.

This is where the commercial license comes in. By purchasing a commercial license, companies can use your software in their proprietary applications without having to release their source code.

### MySQL's Dual-Licensing Model

MySQL is a popular open source database that uses a dual-licensing model. It is available under the GPL, but Oracle (the company that owns MySQL) also sells a commercial license.

This has been a very successful business model for MySQL. It has allowed them to build a large and active community around their software, while also generating significant revenue from commercial licensing.

### Is Dual-Licensing Right for You?

Dual-licensing can be a great way to build a successful open source project. However, it's not right for everyone.

Before you decide to dual-license your project, you should consider the following:

*   **Your Goals:** What do you want to achieve with your project? Are you looking to build a community, generate revenue, or both?
*   **Your Audience:** Who are you trying to reach with your project? Are they developers, businesses, or both?
*   **Your Resources:** Do you have the time and resources to manage a dual-licensing model?

If you're not sure if dual-licensing is right for you, you can always start by releasing your project under a single license (like the GPL) and then add a commercial license later if you decide it's a good fit.

### A Note on "Tivoization"

The GPLv3 was created in part to address a practice known as "tivoization," where hardware manufacturers would use GPL-licensed software on their devices but prevent users from running modified versions of the software on that hardware.

The GPLv2 does not explicitly prohibit tivoization. If this is a concern for you, you may want to consider using the GPLv3 instead of the GPLv2.

However, the GPLv3 is not compatible with the GPLv2. This means that if you license your project under the GPLv3, it cannot be combined with code that is licensed under the GPLv2.

This is a complex issue with a lot of nuance. If you are concerned about tivoization, you should do your own research and consult with a lawyer to decide which license is right for you.
